# Local Cold Calling Playbook

This is a distilled repo-native version of the internal cold call playbook and
walkthrough used to train local-business outbound.

If the motion is software-company or B2B calling, use
[`../b2b-cold-calling/`](../b2b-cold-calling/).

Use this playbook when the list is clean enough to call and the local-business
segment is specific enough that a short phone conversation can create urgency.

The goal of the cold call is not to fully sell. The goal is to:

- verify the prospect is qualified
- show enough relevance to earn interest
- book the meeting

## Before you call

Do not start from a fuzzy list.

Know:

- geography
- business type
- owner or manager title
- pain hypothesis
- why your offer is better
- whether phone numbers are verified

If those are still weak, enrich the list before calling.

## The 3 Steps

There are 3 steps to a successful cold call:

1. building credibility
2. building value
3. booking the meeting

If you fail on one of these, the call usually dies.

## 1. Building credibility

You have about 6 seconds to earn attention.

The opening needs 3 things:

1. Conviction in your voice.
2. Straight to the point.
3. Clear, polished delivery.

That means:

- say who you are immediately
- say why you are calling immediately
- avoid awkward pauses
- avoid stuttering and filler words
- avoid phrases like `um`, `like`, or `you know`
- do not sound sneaky

Prospects are deciding:

- who is this
- do I know them
- do I want to keep talking

Answer those fast.

### Opener template

`Hi {name}, this is {rep} from {company}. I'm calling because we help {segment} with {pain}. Quick question for you.`

Keep the opener short. Do not bury the reason for the call.

Think of this as a handshake through the phone. If the opener feels weak, the
rest of the call usually dies with it.

## 2. Building value

Building value means conveying 3 things:

- you understand their business
- you are credible in the category
- your product is better than the status quo

This is the basic rule:

- people do not care how much you know until they know how much you care

The simplest way to do this is:

1. Ask short questions that expose pain.
2. Listen.
3. Reflect back what you heard in plain English.
4. Connect your offer to that pain in 1-2 sentences.

Do not assume cheaper is enough.

The offer needs to feel meaningfully better overall.

### Qualification prompts

Use short questions like:

- `How are you handling {problem} today?`
- `Is that working the way you want?`
- `What usually slows this down for your team?`
- `Who usually owns this at the business?`

Do not interrogate. The call is not discovery theater.

## 3. Booking the meeting

Once there is enough interest, move to commitment.

The close should do 4 things:

1. define the next step
2. lock a day and time
3. send a calendar invite
4. get the invite accepted while still on the phone

Time kills deals. Same day or within 24 hours is ideal.

Tell the prospect what happens next and what you need from them, if anything.

### Close template

`The next step is a short walkthrough so we can show exactly how this would work for {company}. Are you free later today, or would tomorrow morning be better?`

Once they choose, send the invite immediately.

## Call length

A strong cold call is usually 4-6 minutes.

Do not oversell.

The cold call should:

- confirm fit
- create interest
- earn the meeting

## Objection handling rules

Stay calm and short.

Good pattern:

1. acknowledge
2. re-frame
3. ask permission or one clarifying question
4. tie back to the business problem
5. re-close

Example:

- objection: `Send me something`
- response: `Happy to. Usually when someone asks that, it means either the timing is bad or it isn't a priority yet. Which one is it for you?`

See [`OBJECTION-LIBRARY.md`](./OBJECTION-LIBRARY.md) for common rebuttals.

## Follow-up and gatekeeper handling

Two patterns matter a lot:

- follow-up calls must add new information each time
- gatekeeper handling should stay respectful and direct

## Metrics to watch

Track:

- connection rate
- qualified conversation rate
- meeting-booked rate
- show rate
- close rate

Do not obsess over raw dial count without segment quality.

## Choosing the motion

Use this rule:

- `A` call-first when the local segment is tight, phones are good, and pain is urgent
- `B` enrich first when the list is broad or missing key data
- `C` email first when the segment needs more context before a call makes sense

## What good looks like

- strong first 6 seconds
- no filler
- clear reason for the call
- 1-2 real pain questions
- short value bridge
- definite meeting close
- clear next-step expectation
- calendar invite discipline
- clear problem hook before the value prop

## What bad looks like

- vague intro
- fake confidence
- overexplaining
- pitching before qualification
- no concrete next step
- soft close with no calendar invite
- great discovery but no booked meeting
